WHAT IS THIS?

The Wonder Cabinet is a physical arcade machine built around an unlikely constraint: a single 64×64 RGB LED matrix — just 4,096 pixels. Each pixel is a real, glowing LED. No anti-aliasing, no transparency, no GPU shaders. Every game, simulation, and visualization was designed from scratch to be beautiful at this resolution.

Inside are 55+ playable games, 120+ visual experiences, and 45+ AI-controlled game demos, all running on a Raspberry Pi. The name comes from the Renaissance tradition of Wunderkammern — cabinets of curiosities that gathered natural specimens, scientific instruments, and works of art into a single room of wonder.

The constraint is the point. On a 4K display, a protein molecule is just another render. On 64×64 glowing LEDs, it becomes something you stare at. The resolution forces every visual to be distilled to its essence — what are the minimum pixels needed to recognize a double helix? To see a flock emerge? To feel fire rising?
Controls. Each entry is tagged with its input scheme: D-PAD + ACTION joystick + either button   ACTION ONLY buttons only   D-PAD ONLY joystick only   D-PAD + L / R buttons do different things   L / R BROWSE left/right to cycle

ARCADE

55+ playable games from Pong (1972) to Agar.io (2015), each rebuilt for 64×64 pixels with authentic mechanics.

ARCADE CLASSICS

ASTEROIDS 1979, Atari — Rotate and thrust your ship, shoot asteroids that split into smaller pieces. Screen wraps. Survive and score. Designed by Ed Logg; one of the first games with vector graphics.
BOMBERMAN 1983, Hudson Soft — Place bombs on a grid to destroy walls and enemies. Collect power-ups for range, speed, and extra bombs. Bombs chain-react. Clear all enemies to advance.
BREAKOUT 1976, Atari — Move a paddle to bounce a ball into rows of bricks. Clear all bricks to win. Ball speeds up; paddle shrinks after hitting the ceiling. Steve Wozniak built the prototype.
BURGERTIME 1982, Data East — Walk across burger ingredients to drop them down platforms. Dodge Mr. Hot Dog, Mr. Pickle, and Mr. Egg. Assemble all burgers to clear the level.
CENTIPEDE 1981, Atari — Shoot a centipede weaving down through a mushroom field. Hit a segment and it splits in two. Clear the centipede to advance. Co-designed by Dona Bailey and Ed Logg.
DEFENDER 1981, Williams — Side-scrolling shooter by Eugene Jarvis. Fly left or right, shoot aliens, rescue humanoids from abduction. Radar shows the full map. Hold both buttons for a smart bomb. Popularized the horizontal scrolling shooter.
DIGDUG 1982, Namco — Dig tunnels underground and inflate enemies with your pump until they pop, or lure them under falling rocks. Clear all enemies to advance.
DONKEY KONG 1981, Nintendo — Climb platforms, jump over barrels, and reach the top to rescue Pauline. Mario’s debut (originally “Jumpman”). Shigeru Miyamoto’s first game and the origin of the platformer genre.
FROGGER 1981, Konami — Cross five lanes of traffic, then hop across logs and turtles to reach home. Guide all five frogs home to clear the level. Don’t get hit or fall in the water.
GALAGA 1981, Namco — Fixed-position shooter. Move left/right and fire at alien formations. Let a boss alien capture your ship, then rescue it for double firepower. Survive waves of diving attacks.
INVADERS 1978, Taito — Shoot rows of aliens before they reach the bottom. They speed up as you destroy them — an accidental hardware feature that became the core mechanic. Designed by Tomohiro Nishikado.
LODE RUNNER 1983, Broderbund — Collect all gold on each level, then escape to the top. Dig holes to trap guards. 150 levels. One of the first games with a built-in level editor.
LUNAR LANDER 1979, Atari — Manage thrust and rotation to soft-land on a lunar surface. Land gently on a flat pad to score. Too much speed and you crash.
MS. PAC-MAN 1982, Midway — Eat all dots while avoiding ghosts. Faster than Pac-Man with multiple mazes and semi-random ghost AI. The best-selling American arcade game.
NIGHT DRIVER 1976, Atari — One of the earliest first-person driving games. Steer between roadside posts against a black background. Stay on the road at increasing speeds.
PAC-MAN 1980, Namco — Eat all dots in the maze. Power pellets let you eat ghosts temporarily. Each ghost has unique AI: Blinky chases, Pinky ambushes, Inky flanks, Clyde wanders. Designed by Toru Iwatani.
PONG 1972, Atari — Two paddles, one ball. First to 11 wins. Allan Alcorn built it as a training exercise; Nolan Bushnell put it in a bar. The first commercially successful video game.
Q*BERT 1982, Gottlieb — Hop on cubes in an isometric pyramid to change their colors. Change every cube to advance. Avoid Coily the snake and other enemies. Don’t fall off the edges.
SNAKE 1976 / 1997 — Eat food, grow longer, don’t hit yourself or the walls. Started as Blockade (1976, Gremlin Industries). Nokia’s 1997 version shipped on 400 million phones.
TETRIS 1984, Alexey Pajitnov — Stack falling tetrominoes, clear complete lines. Game ends when pieces reach the top. Created on an Elektronika 60 in Moscow. 520 million copies sold.

RETRO

ARKANOID 1986, Taito — Breakout with power-ups: laser paddle, catch ball, multi-ball, warp gates. Clear all bricks per level. An expanded take on brick-breaking with enemies and a storyline.
DND 1982, Mattel — Explore rooms, fight monsters, collect treasure. Turn-based grid combat inspired by Advanced Dungeons & Dragons: Cloudy Mountain on Intellivision.
INDY 500 1977, Atari — Top-down racing on an oval track. Steer and accelerate to complete laps. One of the original Atari 2600 launch titles.
JEZZBALL 1992, Microsoft — Trap bouncing balls by building walls. Claim 75% of the area to advance. Don’t let a ball hit a wall while it’s forming. Shipped with the Windows Entertainment Pack.
PIPE DREAM 1989, The Assembly Line — Place pipe segments before the water arrives. Build a path long enough to pass the level. Originally released as Pipe Mania; renamed Pipe Dream for the US market by Lucasfilm Games.
SKIFREE 1991, Microsoft — Ski downhill, dodging trees, rocks, and dogs. The Abominable Snowman chases you eventually. By Chris Pirih for Windows.
SPACE CRUISE Side-scrolling starship game inspired by Davidson’s Math Blaster. Navigate asteroid fields, manage shields, fire lasers. Survive as long as possible.
TRASHBLASTER Shoot upward to blast falling garbage before it stacks to the top. Based on the Trash Zapper mini-game from Davidson’s Math Blaster.
3D MONSTR MAZE 1981, Malcolm Evans — Escape a T-Rex stalking you through a first-person 3D maze. Widely considered the first survival horror game, originally on the ZX81.

MODERN

AGAR.IO 2015, Matheus Valadares — Absorb smaller cells, avoid bigger ones. L button boosts speed, R button ejects mass. The browser game that launched the .io genre.
FLAPPY BIRD 2013, Dong Nguyen — Press a button to flap, thread through pipes. One hit and you’re done. So addictive the creator pulled it from the App Store.
GEODASH 2013, RobTop — One-button platformer. Hold the button to jump; the level scrolls at a fixed speed. Hit any obstacle and you restart.
PORTAL 2007, Valve (inspired by) — Navigate puzzle rooms using portals. L button places a blue portal, R button places an orange one. Walk into one, emerge from the other. Reach the exit to clear each room.
SANDGAME 2006, ha55ii / dan-ball.jp (inspired by) — Falling-sand particle physics sandbox. Place 13 materials and watch them interact: sand piles, water flows, fire spreads through oil, gunpowder explodes. No score or goal — a creative toy.
STACK Press the button to drop a sliding block onto the stack. Misalignment shrinks the next platform. Game ends when the block gets too small. Score by stacking as high as possible.
STICK RUNNER Endless runner with a stick figure. Press the button to jump over obstacles. Hold longer to jump higher. Score by distance. Procedurally generated levels.
2048 2014, Gabriele Cirulli — Slide numbered tiles on a 4×4 grid. Matching tiles merge and double. Reach the 2048 tile to win. Game ends when the board fills with no moves left.

TOYS & PUZZLES

BOP IT 1996, Hasbro — Follow commands (bop, twist, pull) using button presses and directional inputs. Commands get faster each round. Miss one and you’re out.
LIGHTS OUT 1995, Tiger Electronics — Press a light and its neighbors toggle. Turn all lights off to win. Solvable with linear algebra over GF(2), but trial and error works too.
MASTERMIND 1971, Mordecai Meirowitz — Guess a hidden color code in limited tries. Each guess gets feedback: right color in right place, or right color in wrong place. Deduce the code before your guesses run out.
RUSH HOUR 1996, ThinkFun — Slide cars and trucks on a grid to free the red car through the exit. Vehicles only move along their length. Puzzles range from easy to very hard.
SIMON 1978, Ralph Baer — Four colored quadrants flash in sequence. Repeat the pattern. Each round adds one step. Game ends when you get the sequence wrong.
15 PUZZLE 1874, Noyes Chapman — Slide 15 numbered tiles into order using the single empty space. Sam Loyd offered $1,000 for solving the “14-15” variant — which is mathematically impossible.

2 PLAYER

CHECKERS ~1100 AD — Jump and capture diagonally. Reach the far side to king a piece. Capture all opponent pieces or block all their moves to win. Solved in 2007 — perfect play always draws.
CHESS ~600 AD — 64 squares, 32 pieces. Checkmate the opponent’s king to win. Each piece type rendered as a recognizable sprite on the LED grid.
CONNECT 4 1974, Milton Bradley — Drop colored discs into columns. Connect four in a row (horizontal, vertical, or diagonal) to win. Solved in 1988 — first player wins with perfect play.
GO ~500 BC — Place black or white stones to surround territory. The player controlling more territory wins. Simple rules, deep strategy. It took until 2016 for a computer (AlphaGo) to beat a top professional player.
MANCALA ~3000 BC — Sow seeds around pits, capture your opponent’s. The player with the most seeds wins. One of the oldest known games, found carved into stone across ancient Africa and the Middle East.
OTHELLO 1973, Goro Hasegawa — Place discs to outflank your opponent’s and flip them to your color. The player with the most discs when the board is full wins. Corner control is key.

BAR GAMES

BOWLING Ten-pin bowling. Time your release and aim your angle. Standard scoring — strikes, spares, and splits over 10 frames.
DARTS 501-style darts with a timing-based throw mechanic. Count down from 501; finish on a double-out. Aim with the joystick, throw with the button.
PINBALL Pinball with flippers, bumpers, ramps, and a plunger. L button controls the left flipper, R button the right. Hit targets to score; don’t let the ball drain.
POOL 8-ball billiards. Aim with the joystick, set power, and shoot. Pocket all your balls (stripes or solids) then sink the 8-ball to win.
SHUFFLEBOARD Slide pucks down a board to land in the highest-scoring zone. Set angle and power. Knock opponents off if you can.

ART

Five well-known paintings as animated LED interpretations. Each captures the composition and palette of the original with movement added — swirling skies, drifting lilies, churning waves.

GREATWAVE Hokusai, c. 1831 — “The Great Wave off Kanagawa.” The cresting wave with Mount Fuji behind it, animated with churning foam and spray. Up/down adjusts animation speed.
MONDRIAN Piet Mondrian, 1930s–40s — Primary-color rectangles in a black grid, in the style of Mondrian’s neoplastic compositions. The grid shifts and recomposes itself. Button generates a new layout.
SCREAM Edvard Munch, 1893 — The figure on the bridge with swirling sky. Animated with rippling colors. Up/down adjusts animation speed.
STARRYNIGHT Vincent van Gogh, 1889 — The swirling night sky over Saint-Rémy. Animated brushstrokes spiral across the panel. Button spawns a shooting star.
WATERLILIES Claude Monet, 1906 — Monet’s Giverny pond with floating lilies and reflected sky. Lilies drift slowly. Button adds a ripple.

AUTOMATA

Cellular automata and agent simulations. Simple local rules produce complex global behavior — spirals, flocks, spots, waves — all emerging from randomness with no central control.

AURORA Particles streaming along magnetic field lines, producing shimmering bands of green, violet, and cyan. Inspired by the Northern Lights.
BOIDS Craig Reynolds, 1987 — Three rules per agent: stay close, match heading, don’t collide. Flocking behavior emerges with no central control. The same algorithm is used in film VFX, robotics, and crowd simulation.
CRYSTALLIZE R.B. Potts, 1952 — Competing crystal domains grow from random seeds using Monte Carlo dynamics. Grain boundaries form where domains meet. Based on the Potts model — a generalization of the Ising model to multiple spin states.
CYCLIC Each state can only be consumed by the next state in the cycle. Produces rotating spirals and expanding amoeba-like structures. Also known as Demon Spirals.
FADERS Cells glow, fade, and reactivate based on their neighbors' brightness. Produces cascading pulses and traveling waves of light.
GYRE Rotating vortex patterns driven by simple agent rules. Particles orbit local centers of rotation, forming spirals that merge and split.
HODGE Gerhardt & Schuster — Models the Belousov-Zhabotinsky chemical reaction, a real oscillator that produces spiral waves in a petri dish. Self-organizing spirals emerge from random initial conditions.
LIFE John Conway, 1970 — Four rules about birth, survival, and death produce gliders, oscillators, and self-replicating structures. Proven Turing-complete. The most famous cellular automaton.
LENIA Bert Chan, 2018 — Continuous cellular automaton that produces lifelike self-organizing patterns. Smooth kernel functions replace discrete neighbor counts. Creatures emerge, move, interact, and die — artificial life from pure math.
MITOSIS A simulation of cell division. Cells grow, duplicate their contents, pinch in half, and separate.
PARTICLE LIFE Jeffrey Ventrella, 2007 — Particles with random attraction and repulsion matrices self-organize into clusters, chains, and orbits. Simple force tables produce startlingly lifelike emergent behavior.
QUARKS Particles with charge-like properties that attract and repel, forming fleeting bonds and structures. Simple local rules produce behavior reminiscent of particle physics.
RUG An averaging cellular automaton. Each cell becomes the average of its neighbors, then saturates, producing symmetric patterns that look like woven rugs.
SANDPILE Bak, Tang & Wiesenfeld, 1987 — The Abelian Sandpile Model. Drop grains one at a time; chain-reaction avalanches form fractal patterns. Demonstrates self-organized criticality — the mechanism behind power laws in earthquakes and markets.
SLIME Jeff Jones, 2010 — Agents deposit and follow chemical trails, mimicking slime mold (Physarum polycephalum). Simple deposit-sense-turn rules produce networks similar to real transport systems.
STARWARS A totalistic cellular automaton with birth/survival rules. Produces long-lived oscillating structures and traveling sparks.
WOLFRAM Stephen Wolfram, 1983 — 256 elementary 1D cellular automata, each defined by a single number. Rule 30 generates randomness; Rule 110 is Turing-complete.

DIGITAL

Demoscene effects and procedural graphics. Many of these techniques come from the 1980s tradition of programmers competing to create real-time visual effects in minimal code.

AMIGA BARS Metallic gradient bars bouncing on sine waves. Uses the Amiga copper chip technique — changing colors mid-scanline. A signature effect of late-80s demos.
DRIFT 3D Comanche-style voxel space terrain flyover. Height maps and color maps rendered with ray marching to produce rolling landscapes, water, and atmospheric fog.
FLUX Particle streams guided by time-varying vector fields. Particles trace smooth curves, creating ribbons of color.
FROZEN LAKE A frozen black lake under a dark sky. Derived from convergent imagery that emerged independently across multiple AI imagination experiments.
LARSON SCAN A bright dot sweeping back and forth with a fading trail — the Cylon eye from Battlestar Galactica (and Kit from Knight Rider). Named after producer Glen A. Larson.
MATRIX Falling green characters with trail fading, from the film’s “digital rain” effect. Designed by Simon Whiteley using characters scanned from a Japanese cookbook.
MAZE First-person maze navigation in the style of the Windows 95 screensaver. The camera auto-navigates through corridors and intersections.
MOBIUS A rotating Möbius strip — the one-sided surface discovered in 1858. 3D wireframe or shaded projection spinning in space.
MOIRE Two overlapping grids, slightly offset and rotating, produce shimmering moiré interference patterns.
RAINBOW Cycling HSV color gradients sweeping across the panel.
ROTOZOOM Simultaneous rotation and zoom of a procedural texture using rotation matrix math. A staple of Amiga and Atari ST demos.
SINESCROLL Text scrolling along a sine wave — the classic demoscene intro effect. Each character bobs independently on a sinusoidal path.
TRANCE Pulsing concentric shapes and strobing color patterns forming an expanding/contracting tunnel effect.
TRUCHET Sébastien Truchet, 1704 — Quarter-circle tiles placed randomly create flowing organic curves. Discovered by a Dominican priest over 300 years ago.
TWISTER Vertical bars twist as if wrapped around a spinning cylinder. A demoscene staple creating depth from 2D math.
XOR XOR the x and y coordinates of each pixel, map the result to color. A single bitwise operation produces Sierpinski-like recursive patterns.

MECHANICS

Animated mechanical models from ancient astronomical instruments to Industrial Revolution engines. Each shows the actual working mechanism — gears meshing, pistons firing, shuttles flying.

ANTIKYTHERA c. 100 BC — Ancient Greek analog computer with interlocking bronze gears that predicted astronomical positions and eclipses. Nothing comparable was built for 1,500 years.
ARCHIMEDES c. 250 BC — A helical surface inside a cylinder, turned by hand to raise water. Still used today for irrigation and wastewater.
ASTROLABE c. 200 BC – 1600s — A brass instrument for telling time, finding latitude, predicting sunrise/sunset, and locating stars. Used by astronomers and navigators for over a thousand years.
BEAM ENGINE 1769 — Watt’s separate condenser transformed Newcomen’s earlier engine into the workhorse of the Industrial Revolution. The rocking beam links piston to flywheel.
CAMSHAFT A rotating camshaft with eccentric lobes operating valve followers. Converts rotation into precisely timed reciprocating motion — the basis of internal combustion valve timing.
CURTA 1948 — Curt Herzstark's handheld mechanical calculator, designed while imprisoned in Buchenwald. Cylindrical Leibniz wheels inside a pepper-grinder housing.
GRANDFATHER Christiaan Huygens, 1656 — Longcase pendulum clock with escapement mechanism. The pendulum swings, the escape wheel ticks, the hands turn.
GUTENBERG c. 1440 — Movable type printing press. The full mechanical cycle: inking, bed slide, press down, impression, release.
GYROSCOPE A spinning rotor that resists changes to its orientation. Demonstrates precession, nutation, and conservation of angular momentum.
JACQUARD 1804 — Punch cards controlling a weaving loom — the direct ancestor of computer programming. Babbage modeled his Analytical Engine on its logic; Lovelace saw the deeper computational implications.
LOCOMOTIVE Side-view cutaway with driving wheels, coupling rods, and crankshaft. The crank-slider mechanism converts the piston's linear motion into rotary motion.
LOOM A working weaving loom. The shuttle passes through the warp threads, the heddles lift, the beater presses. Cloth forms row by row.
MODEL T 1908, Henry Ford — Animated cross-section showing the engine, transmission, and drive train of the car that introduced mass production to automobiles.
ORRERY 1713 — A clockwork model of the solar system. Brass arms carry planets at their correct relative orbital speeds, with gears visible at the base. Named after the 4th Earl of Orrery.
PROJECTOR A film projector with spinning reels, film threading through the gate, and a beam of light. 24 still images per second creating the illusion of motion.
SINGER A sewing machine with needle, bobbin, and feed dog. The needle plunges, the thread loops, the fabric advances.
TYPEWRITER A mechanical typewriter with type bars striking an inked ribbon against paper. The carriage advances, the bell rings at the margin, the return lever sweeps back.
WATCH A five-gear train: barrel → center → third → fourth → escapement. Balance wheel with hairspring, proper gear meshing, and clock hands. Mechanical timekeeping in miniature.

HURDY GURDY Medieval string instrument where a rosined wheel acts as a continuous bow. Keys press tangents against the strings. Drone notes sustain while the melody plays above.

OUTDOORS

Natural phenomena computed from first principles — fire, water, weather, and starlight.

CONSTELLATIONS Real star positions from the Hipparcos catalog (ESA, 1997) with constellation lines from Stellarium. Stars rendered at correct relative brightness. Joystick to rotate the sky.
DRIFT Procedurally generated terrain with water, sand, grass, and snow. A landscape sandbox with day/night cycle.
FIRE Classic fire simulation with a heat source at the bottom, upward propagation, and cooling. The algorithm dates back to the demoscene of the early 1990s.
FIREFLIES Kuramoto model of coupled oscillators. Each firefly has its own internal clock but synchronizes with visible neighbors — the same behavior seen in real firefly swarms.
JELLYFISH Jeffrey Ventrella, 2007 — Particle Life variant tuned for drifting, pulsing jellyfish motion. Multiple species with asymmetric attraction and repulsion forces produce bell shapes trailing tentacles.
LAKE A lake surface with ripples, reflections, and gentle wave motion. Pixels shimmer as if catching light on water.
MOON PHASES The lunar cycle animated with astronomically correct illumination. The terminator sweeps across the Moon’s surface from new to full and back.
PLASMA Four overlapping sine functions create smoothly animated color fields. One of the quintessential demoscene effects.
RIPPLES Concentric rings expand from random drop points, interact, and fade. A rain-on-water simulation using the 2D wave equation with damping. Interference patterns form where rings collide.
STARFIELD 3D stars rushing toward you with perspective projection. Dots start small in the center and streak outward as they approach.
WEATHER Animated weather scenes: rain, snow, thunderstorms, sunshine. Particles fall with realistic physics, lightning flashes illuminate the grid.

ROAD + RAIL

Traffic simulations modeling real dynamics — congestion waves, signal timing, merge behavior.

CLOVERLEAF A highway interchange with looping ramps. Cars flow through the cloverleaf pattern, merging and diverging without stopping.
FLAGS National flags of the world rendered on the LED matrix. 200+ flags loaded from PNG bitmaps. Joystick to browse; overlay shows country name. Data from flagcdn.com.
GREENWAVE Traffic signals timed so that a car traveling at the speed limit hits green lights all the way down a corridor.
GRIDLOCK Biham, Middleton & Levine, 1992 — The BML traffic model. Cars move east or north on alternating steps. Below a critical density, traffic flows. Above it, the system jams completely. A phase transition you can see.
HIGHWAY Multi-lane highway traffic with lane changes, speed differences, and phantom traffic jams. Congestion waves propagate backward through the flow — the accordion effect made visible.
ONRAMP Cars merging from a ramp onto a flowing highway. Gap acceptance, yield behavior, and the cascade effects of a single slow merge.
ROAD A single road with cars following each other. Car-following models produce stop-and-go waves from nothing — no accident, no bottleneck, just the physics of human reaction times.
ROTARY A traffic roundabout with entering and circulating vehicles. Yield-on-entry dynamics create a self-regulating flow. The European alternative to the four-way stop.
SIGNALS A signalized intersection with timed red/green phases. Cars queue, the light changes, the queue discharges.
SIGNS Street signs from around the world — stop signs, speed limits, yield signs, and highway markers from 6 continents. 63 signs across 6 categories.

SCIENCE

Physics, chemistry, and biology simulations solving real equations in real time — Navier-Stokes for fluids, Coulomb’s law for fields, the wave equation for diffraction.

ATTRACTORS Edward Lorenz, 1963 — The Lorenz “butterfly” attractor, the Rössler attractor, and the Thomas attractor. Trajectories that fold through space without ever crossing themselves.
BRAIN Izhikevich model — A spiking neural network with excitatory and inhibitory neurons. Regular spiking, chattering, and bursting patterns. Membrane potential dynamics with real spike generation.
CELL A data-driven cross-section of a living cell. 14 real biochemical pathways: ATP synthase spinning, ribosomes translating mRNA, protein complexes cycling through conformational states.
CHLADNI Ernst Chladni, 1787 — Sand on a vibrating plate collects at the nodes, revealing standing wave patterns. Each frequency produces a unique geometric figure.
COLOR MIX Computational fluid dynamics with colored dyes mixing in a flow field. Pigments swirl, fold, and blend according to Navier-Stokes equations.
COULOMB Coulomb electrostatics with field-line tracing and equipotential rendering. Five scenarios — dipole, quadrupole, motor, circuit, and random — all governed by the inverse-square law.
DBL PENDULUM Two arms, two masses, deterministic equations — yet the trajectory never repeats. The simplest physical system that produces chaos.
DNA The central dogma of molecular biology, animated. Six scenarios: double helix structure, replication, transcription, codon reading, mutation, and chromatin packing.
EARTH A rotating globe showing continents, oceans, and the terminator (day/night boundary). The planet as seen from space, rendered on a 64×64 pixel sphere.
FRACTALS Classic fractals rendered in real time: Mandelbrot set, Julia sets, Sierpinski triangle, Koch snowflake, and fractal trees. Joystick to pan and zoom into infinite self-similar detail.
GRAY-SCOTT Alan Turing, 1952 — Turing proposed that reaction-diffusion could explain biological pattern formation. The Gray-Scott model uses two parameters: F (feed rate, how fast chemical U is replenished) and K (kill rate, how fast chemical V is removed). Different F/K combinations produce distinct pattern families — spots, stripes, coral, and worms. The joystick explores the Pearson parameter space; overlay labels show the Greek-letter classification (Alpha, Beta, etc.) from Pearson’s 1993 taxonomy.
INK DROPS Drops of colored ink falling into a fluid simulation. The ink spreads, curls, and diffuses according to Navier-Stokes equations.
LATTICES 3D rotating unit cells of real crystalline structures: diamond, salt, ice, metals. Tiled cells with edge fading show infinite periodicity.
MICROSCOPE Scenes viewed through a virtual microscope: microorganisms, cell structures, and crystal formations.
MATTER The three phases of matter — solid, liquid, and gas — simulated with interacting particles. Temperature controls phase transitions: melting, boiling, condensation, and freezing made visible.
MOLECULES 3D ball-and-stick models with CPK coloring (carbon black, oxygen red, nitrogen blue). Real molecules rotating in space: amino acids, sugars, neurotransmitters.
OPTICS Geometric ray tracing from first principles. Light enters a prism and splits into spectral colors at their correct refractive indices. Other scenarios show lenses, mirrors, and kaleidoscopes.
OSCILLOSCOPE Phosphor-green vintage oscilloscope with sine, square, triangle, and sawtooth waveforms. Lissajous mode draws figures from frequency ratios — the same patterns used to calibrate TV broadcasts.
PERIODIC Animated Bohr models for all 118 elements. Electrons orbit on shells, nuclei pulse. Element categories color-coded: alkali metals, noble gases, transition metals, lanthanides.
PROTEINS Real protein backbone structures from the RCSB Protein Data Bank. Cα coordinates with color-coded secondary structure: helices in red, sheets in yellow, coils in gray.
RADIOACTIVE A grid of atoms spontaneously decays, emitting alpha, beta, and gamma particles. Five scenarios demonstrate different decay modes. Each event is probabilistic — quantum randomness made visible.
SOLAR SYSTEM Newtonian gravity with Velocity Verlet integration. Planets orbit at correct relative speeds around a central star.
SPACE DUST An asteroid belt of particles orbiting under gravity. Rocks drift, collide, and cluster. Softened gravitational potential prevents singularities at close approach.
SPECTROSCOPE Real element emission spectra at their correct wavelengths. Each element has a unique fingerprint of spectral lines — this is how we know what distant stars are made of.
TURING Alan Turing, 1952 — Reaction-diffusion patterns computed on a grid. Four pattern families: spots, stripes, coral, and worms. The mechanism Turing proposed for how leopards get their spots.
WATER CYCLE The hydrological cycle animated: evaporation, cloud formation, precipitation, runoff, and groundwater flow. A continuous loop of water moving through the Earth system.
WAVE TANK The 2D wave equation solved with leapfrog integration. Point sources, double-slit diffraction, two-source interference, ripples, and reflection. Real wave physics on a grid.
WIND TUNNEL Jos Stam, SIGGRAPH 1999 — Computational fluid dynamics with obstacles. Semi-Lagrangian advection shows vortex shedding, turbulence, and flow separation around objects. Real aerodynamics at 64×64.
3 BODY PROBLEM Three bodies orbiting under mutual gravitational attraction. No closed-form solution exists — the system is inherently chaotic. Watch them dance, fling each other away, and sometimes find temporary stability.

HOW IT WORKS

64x64 LED Array + Raspberry Pi + Joystick + 2 Buttons + Power Supply

DISPLAY 64×64 RGB LED matrix
HUB75 interface
4,096 individually addressable LEDs
COMPUTER Raspberry Pi 3
1.2 GHz ARM Cortex-A53
1 GB RAM
SOFTWARE Python 3
~40,000 lines of code
Every effect built from scratch
INPUT Joystick + 2 buttons
Wired to GPIO pins
Hold both to exit
No GPU. No shaders. Every pixel is computed in Python on a 1.2 GHz ARM chip. Fluid dynamics, ray tracing, classic arcade games, reaction-diffusion, N-body gravity — all running at 30fps on tiny, concise physical hardware.
