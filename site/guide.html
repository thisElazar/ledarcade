WHAT IS THIS?

The Wonder Cabinet is a physical arcade machine built around an unlikely constraint: a single 64×64 RGB LED matrix — just 4,096 pixels. Each pixel is a real, glowing LED. No anti-aliasing, no transparency, no GPU shaders. Every game, simulation, and visualization was designed from scratch to be beautiful at this resolution.

Inside are 54 playable games, 119 visual experiences, and 44 AI-controlled game demos, all running on a Raspberry Pi. The name comes from the Renaissance tradition of Wunderkammern — cabinets of curiosities that gathered natural specimens, scientific instruments, and works of art into a single room of wonder.

The constraint is the point. On a 4K display, a protein molecule is just another render. On 64×64 glowing LEDs, it becomes something you stare at. The resolution forces every visual to be distilled to its essence — what are the minimum pixels needed to recognize a double helix? To see a flock emerge? To feel fire rising?

THE ARCADE

54 playable games spanning the full history of the medium — from Pong (1972) to Agar.io (2015). Every game reimagined for 4,096 pixels with authentic mechanics. The golden age of arcades, shrunk to a grid you can hold in your hands.

ARCADE CLASSICS

ASTEROIDS 1979, Atari — Vector graphics in a wrap-around arena. Shoot rocks, they split into smaller rocks. Elegant physics: thrust, inertia, rotation. Ed Logg's masterpiece of minimalism.
BOMBERMAN 1983, Hudson Soft — Lay bombs on a grid, destroy walls, collect power-ups. Bombs chain-react. Timing and positioning are everything. The original one-more-game loop.
BREAKOUT 1976, Atari — Steve Wozniak built the prototype; Steve Jobs sold it. Eight rows of colored bricks, authentic speed-up mechanics, paddle shrinks when the ball hits the ceiling.
BURGERTIME 1982, Data East — Chef Peter Pepper walks across burger ingredients to assemble them while dodging Mr. Hot Dog, Mr. Pickle, and Mr. Egg. One of the strangest premises in arcade history.
CENTIPEDE 1981, Atari — Dona Bailey, one of the first women in game development, co-designed this trackball shooter. A centipede weaves down through a mushroom field; hit a segment and it splits in two.
DEFENDER 1981, Williams — Eugene Jarvis's side-scrolling shooter with a scrolling radar display. Protect humanoids from alien abduction. Famously difficult, famously rewarding. The game that popularized the horizontal scrolling shooter.
DIG DUG 1982, Namco — Dig tunnels through the earth and inflate underground monsters until they pop. Or drop rocks on them. A uniquely satisfying mechanic that has never been replicated.
DONKEY KONG 1981, Nintendo — Mario's debut (he was called “Jumpman”). Shigeru Miyamoto's first game. The cabinet where a plumber, a gorilla, and a damsel created the platformer genre.
FROGGER 1981, Konami — Cross five lanes of traffic, then hop across logs and turtles to reach home. Simple rules, infinite tension. One of the best-selling arcade games of all time.
GALAGA 1981, Namco — The risk-reward dual-ship capture mechanic: let the aliens kidnap your fighter, then rescue it for double firepower. Formation attacks and diving runs. The pinnacle of fixed shooters.
SPACE INVADERS 1978, Taito — Tomohiro Nishikado's game caused a 100-yen coin shortage in Japan. The aliens speed up as you destroy them — an accidental feature that became the defining mechanic.
LODE RUNNER 1983, Brøderbund — Dig holes in platforms to trap guards, collect all the gold, escape to the top. One of the first games with a level editor. 150 levels of pure puzzle-platforming.
LUNAR LANDER 1979, Atari — Manage thrust and rotation to soft-land on a craggy lunar surface. Physics-based gameplay before the term existed. Gentle on the throttle or you crater.
MS. PAC-MAN 1982, Midway — The sequel that many consider superior. Faster, with more mazes and semi-random ghost behavior. The best-selling American arcade game of all time.
NIGHT DRIVER 1976, Atari — One of the earliest first-person driving games. White posts fly past against a black void. No road texture, no horizon — just speed and reflexes in the dark.
PAC-MAN 1980, Namco — Toru Iwatani wanted to attract women to arcades. He designed a game about eating. Each ghost has a unique personality: Blinky chases, Pinky ambushes, Inky flanks, Clyde wanders. The first game character.
PONG 1972, Atari — Where it all began. Allan Alcorn built it as a training exercise. Nolan Bushnell put it in a bar. The coin box overflowed. An industry was born.
Q*BERT 1982, Gottlieb — An isometric pyramid of cubes. Hop on each one to change its color while avoiding Coily the snake. The speech balloon of swear symbols when you fall off is iconic.
SNAKE 1976 / 1997 — The snake genre began as Blockade, a 1976 coin-op by Gremlin Industries — two players walling each other off in real time. Nokia's Taneli Armanto reimagined it as a solo game for the Nokia 6110 in 1997: eat food, grow longer, don't hit yourself. Pre-installed on 400 million phones, it became the most-played game before smartphones.
TETRIS 1984, Alexey Pajitnov — Created on an Elektronika 60 in Moscow. Stack tetrominoes, clear lines, chase the long piece. The subject of a Cold War licensing battle. Sold 520 million copies.

RETRO

ARKANOID 1986, Taito — Breakout reborn with power-ups, enemies, and a storyline. Laser paddle, catch ball, multi-ball, warp gates. The game that proved the brick-breaking genre had room to grow.
DND 1982, Mattel — Inspired by Advanced Dungeons & Dragons: Cloudy Mountain on Intellivision. Explore rooms, fight monsters, collect treasure. Turn-based combat on a grid — console dungeon-crawling distilled to 64×64 pixels.
INDY 500 1977, Atari — Top-down racing on an oval track. Simple controls, tight cornering, pure speed. One of the original Atari 2600 launch titles.
JEZZBALL 1992, Microsoft — Trap bouncing balls by building walls. Claim 75% of the area to advance. Shipped with Windows Entertainment Pack. A puzzle game that rewards patience and spatial thinking.
PIPE DREAM 1989, The Assembly Line — Place pipe segments before the water arrives. A frantic real-time puzzle where you’re always building just ahead of disaster. Originally released as Pipe Mania; renamed Pipe Dream for the US market by Lucasfilm Games.
SKIFREE 1991, Microsoft — Chris Pirih's beloved Windows ski game. Dodge trees, rocks, and dogs. Then the Abominable Snowman appears and eats you. Everyone remembers the snowman.
SPACE CRUISE A side-scrolling starship odyssey inspired by Davidson's Math Blaster. Navigate asteroid fields, manage shields, fire lasers — the number-crunching stripped away, the arcade action kept.
TRASHBLASTER Shoot upward to blast falling garbage before it stacks too high. Based on the Trash Zapper mini-game from Davidson's Math Blaster, distilled to pure arcade reflex.
3D MONSTER MAZE 1981, Malcolm Evans — Widely considered the first survival horror game. Escape a T-Rex stalking you through a 3D maze, originally on the ZX81 using character-block 3D rendering. Recreated in first-person on 64×64 pixels.

MODERN

AGAR.IO 2015, Matheus Valadares — Absorb smaller cells, avoid bigger ones. The browser game that launched the .io genre. Exponential growth, split mechanics, and the perpetual fear of something larger.
FLAPPY BIRD 2013, Dong Nguyen — Tap to flap, thread through pipes. So maddeningly addictive that the creator pulled it from the App Store. Proof that the simplest mechanic can be the most compelling.
GEOMETRY DASH 2013, RobTop — One-button rhythm platformer. Tap to jump in sync with the geometry. The obstacle course scrolls at a fixed speed — memorize, react, repeat.
PORTAL 2007, Valve (inspired by) — Place orange and blue portals to navigate puzzle rooms. Walk into one, emerge from the other. Spatial thinking with the logic turned inside out.
SAND GAME 2006, ha55ii / dan-ball.jp (inspired by) — Falling sand particle physics sandbox. Place 13 materials and watch them interact: sand piles, water flows, fire spreads through oil, gunpowder explodes, acid dissolves, lava converts stone, ice freezes water. A creative toy with no score and no goal — just physics.
STACK Time your drops to stack blocks perfectly. Each misalignment shrinks the next platform. Simple, meditative, and relentlessly punishing as the tower grows.
STICK RUNNER An endless runner with a stick figure protagonist. Jump, duck, and dodge obstacles in a procedurally generated world. How far can you go?
2048 2014, Gabriele Cirulli — Slide numbered tiles on a grid. Matching tiles merge and double. Chase the 2048 tile. A weekend project that went viral and spawned a thousand clones.

TOYS & PUZZLES

BOP IT 1996, Hasbro — Bop it! Twist it! Pull it! A reflex game that barks commands faster and faster. The cabinet version uses button presses and directional inputs.
LIGHTS OUT 1995, Tiger Electronics — Press a light and its neighbors toggle. Turn them all off. A puzzle with an elegant mathematical solution involving linear algebra over GF(2). Simple to play, deep to solve.
MASTERMIND 1971, Mordecai Meirowitz — Guess a hidden color code in limited tries. Each guess gets feedback: right color right place, right color wrong place. Information theory as a game.
RUSH HOUR 1996, ThinkFun — Slide cars and trucks on a grid to free the red car. A spatial logic puzzle with solutions ranging from trivial to fiendish. Pure constraint satisfaction.
SIMON 1978, Ralph Baer — Four colored quadrants flash in sequence. Repeat the pattern. Each round adds one more. A test of short-term memory that never gets easier.
15 PUZZLE 1874, Noyes Chapman — Slide 15 numbered tiles to put them in order. Sam Loyd offered $1,000 for solving the “14-15” variant — which is mathematically impossible. Group theory in a toy.

2 PLAYER

CHECKERS ~1100 AD — Jump and capture on a diagonal grid. Solved by computer in 2007 — perfect play from both sides always ends in a draw. Demo mode emulates Arthur Samuel’s 1959 IBM program — a pioneer of machine learning through self-play.
CHESS ~600 AD — The royal game. 64 squares, 32 pieces, and more possible positions than atoms in the observable universe. The cabinet renders each piece as a recognizable sprite on the LED grid. Demo mode emulates Alex Bernstein’s 1957 IBM program — one of the first complete chess programs.
CONNECT 4 1974, Milton Bradley — Drop colored discs into columns, connect four in a row. Solved in 1988 — first player always wins with perfect play. Simple enough for children, deep enough for study.
GO ~500 BC — The oldest board game still played in its original form. Place stones to surround territory. Simple rules, bottomless strategy. It took until 2016 for a computer (AlphaGo) to beat the world champion.
MANCALA ~3000 BC — The oldest known game. Sow seeds around pits, capture your opponent's. Found carved into stone in ancient Egypt, played across every continent. Mathematics of distribution and timing.
OTHELLO 1973, Goro Hasegawa — Place discs to outflank your opponent’s and flip them to your color. “A minute to learn, a lifetime to master.” Corner control is everything. Demo mode plays with positional strategy and alpha-beta search.

BAR GAMES

BOWLING Ten-pin bowling with realistic pin physics. Time your release, aim your angle. Strikes, spares, and splits on a 64-pixel lane.
DARTS 501-style darts. A moving cursor and timing-based throws. Hit the bullseye, chase the doubles, finish on a double-out. Pub precision in pixel form.
PINBALL A miniature pinball table with flippers, bumpers, ramps, and a plunger. Real ball physics at 64×64 resolution. Hit the targets, rack up the score, don't drain.
POOL Billiards with real 2D physics. Aim, set power, break. Ball collisions with proper momentum transfer on a pocket-sized felt table.
SHUFFLEBOARD Slide pucks down a long board to score in the highest-value zone. Power and aim. The bar game of kings, miniaturized.

FAMOUS PAINTINGS

Five of the world's most recognizable paintings, re-imagined as animated LED interpretations. Not reproductions — living, breathing pixel versions that capture the essence of each work.

GREAT WAVE Hokusai, c. 1831 — “The Great Wave off Kanagawa.” The cresting wave with Mount Fuji behind it, animated with churning foam and spray. Japan's most famous artwork on 4,096 LEDs.
MONDRIAN Piet Mondrian, 1930s–40s — Primary colors in a black grid. Neoplasticism rendered at the resolution it was almost designed for. The grid shifts and recomposes itself.
SCREAM Edvard Munch, 1893 — The swirling sky, the bridge, the figure with hands on face. Expressionist anguish distilled to pixel art. The sky ripples with animated anxiety.
STARRY NIGHT Vincent van Gogh, 1889 — The swirling night sky over Saint-Rémy. Animated brushstrokes spiral across the panel, the village glows below, and the cypress tree reaches upward.
WATER LILIES Claude Monet, 1906 — Monet's Giverny pond with floating lilies and reflected sky. Impressionist color mixing rendered as gently drifting pixels. Peaceful and meditative.

THE RULES OF LIFE

Drop a few simple rules into a grid. Press play. Watch complexity emerge from nothing. These simulations explore one of the deepest questions in science: how does order arise from chaos? Each starts from randomness and organizes itself — spirals, flocks, spots, waves — with no central control.

AURORA Curtains of light undulating across the grid, inspired by the Northern Lights. Particles stream along magnetic field lines, producing shimmering green, violet, and cyan bands.
BOIDS Craig Reynolds, 1987 — Three rules: stay close, match heading, don't collide. From these alone, flocking emerges. The same algorithm is now used in film VFX, robotics, and crowd simulation. No bird knows the plan. The flock has no leader.
CYCLIC A cyclic cellular automaton where each state can only be consumed by the next state in the cycle. Produces rotating spirals and expanding amoeba-like structures. Also known as Demon Spirals.
FADERS Cells glow, fade, and reactivate based on their neighbors' brightness. Produces cascading pulses and traveling waves of light that sweep across the grid in organic patterns.
GYRE Rotating vortex patterns driven by simple agent rules. Particles orbit local centers of rotation, forming hypnotic spiral structures that merge and split.
HODGEPODGE Gerhardt & Schuster — A cellular automaton that models the Belousov-Zhabotinsky chemical reaction — a real chemical oscillator that produces spiral waves in a petri dish. The same self-organizing spirals appear here, emerging from random initial conditions.
GAME OF LIFE John Conway, 1970 — The most famous cellular automaton. Four rules about birth, survival, and death produce an endless menagerie of gliders, oscillators, and self-replicating structures. Proven Turing-complete.
MITOSIS A simulation of cell division. Cells grow, duplicate their contents, pinch in half, and separate. The fundamental process of all life, animated on the grid.
QUARKS Particles with charge-like properties that attract and repel, forming fleeting bonds and structures. Inspired by particle physics but governed by simple local interaction rules.
RUG An averaging cellular automaton that produces Persian-rug-like symmetric patterns. Each cell becomes the average of its neighbors, then saturates, creating kaleidoscopic woven textures.
SANDPILE Bak, Tang & Wiesenfeld, 1987 — The Abelian Sandpile Model. Drop grains one at a time. Watch chain-reaction avalanches form fractal patterns. This is self-organized criticality — the mechanism behind power laws in earthquakes and markets.
SLIME Jeff Jones, 2010 — Agents that deposit and follow chemical trails, mimicking slime mold (Physarum polycephalum). From simple deposit-sense-turn rules, networks form that are eerily similar to the Tokyo rail system.
STAR WARS CA A totalistic cellular automaton with birth/survival rules inspired by the Star Wars universe. Produces complex, long-lived patterns of oscillating structures and traveling sparks.
WOLFRAM Stephen Wolfram, 1983 — 256 elementary cellular automata, each defined by a single number. Rule 30 is used as a random number generator. Rule 110 is Turing-complete — a 1D grid that can compute anything.

DIGITAL DREAMS

The demoscene is an underground computer art movement born in the 1980s — programmers competing to create the most beautiful real-time effects in the smallest possible code. Many of these techniques come directly from that tradition.

AMIGA BARS Metallic horizontal gradient bars bouncing on sine waves. A tribute to the Amiga copper chip — a coprocessor that could change colors mid-scanline. The signature effect of late-80s demos.
FLUX Flowing particle streams guided by time-varying vector fields. Particles trace smooth curves through space, creating ribbons of color that ebb and surge.
LARSON SCAN The sweeping red eye of a Cylon from Battlestar Galactica (and Kit from Knight Rider). A single bright dot bounces back and forth with a fading trail. Also called a Larson Scanner after Glen A. Larson.
MATRIX RAIN Falling green characters with trail fading, inspired by the film's “digital rain” effect. Designed by Simon Whiteley, who scanned his wife's Japanese cookbook for the characters.
MAZE A 3D maze screensaver in the style of Windows 95. First-person perspective as the camera navigates through corridors, making turns at intersections. Pure nostalgia.
MOBIUS A rotating Möbius strip — the surface with only one side. 3D projection of the twisted band, rendered as a wireframe or shaded surface spinning in space.
MOIRE Overlapping line patterns create shimmering moiré interference fringes. Two grids, slightly offset and rotating, produce organic flowing shapes from pure geometry.
RAINBOW Sweeping HSV color gradients across the entire panel. Simple and hypnotic — a cycling rainbow that fills the grid with smoothly shifting hues.
ROTOZOOM Simultaneous rotation and zoom of a procedural texture using rotation matrix math. A staple of Amiga and Atari ST demos. The texture wraps infinitely.
SINE SCROLLER Text scrolling along a sine wave. The classic demoscene intro effect — a message undulates across the screen, each character bobbing independently on a sinusoidal path.
TRANCE Pulsing concentric shapes and strobing color patterns. A hypnotic tunnel of expanding and contracting geometric forms synced to an internal rhythm.
TRUCHET TILES Sébastien Truchet, 1704 — Quarter-circle tiles placed randomly create flowing organic curves. A Dominican priest's mathematical observation, over 300 years old and still mesmerizing.
TWISTER A rotating 3D column effect. Vertical bars twist and warp as if wrapped around a spinning cylinder. Another demoscene staple, creating the illusion of depth from 2D math.
XOR PATTERN XOR the x and y coordinates of each pixel and map the result to color. A single bitwise operation produces intricate recursive patterns reminiscent of Sierpinski triangles.

DOMESTIC NOSTALGIA

The visual detritus of everyday life, elevated. Screensavers you grew up with, toys you forgot you loved, the ambient background of domestic existence — given a second life in pixels.

CAT A pixel cat doing pixel cat things. Walking, sitting, sleeping, chasing a laser dot. The internet's favorite animal, rendered at its most essential resolution.
CLOCK A working digital clock. Hours, minutes, and seconds displayed on the LED matrix. Functional, beautiful, and a reminder that this is, at its heart, a computer.
DVD c. 2000 — The iconic bouncing DVD logo screensaver. Color changes on every wall hit. The whole screen flashes when it hits the corner. You know you've been waiting for it.
LAVA LAMP Edward Craven Walker, 1963 — Wax blobs rise and fall through heated liquid. Metaballs simulate the organic splitting and merging. The definitive ambient decoration of the 1960s and '70s.
NEWTON'S CRADLE Five suspended steel balls transferring momentum. Pull one back, release, and watch the energy pass through. Conservation of momentum and energy, made kinetic and meditative.
POLAROID An instant camera that “photographs” other visuals in the collection. The image develops slowly in the classic Polaroid white frame. Nostalgia inside nostalgia.
AQUARIUM A tropical fish tank rendered in pixels. Eight species school loosely through swaying plants, bubbles rise from the substrate, and caustic light patterns shimmer on the gravel. Three color schemes — Tropical, Moonlight, and Sunset — turn the display into a living window.
SOLITAIRE 1990, Wes Cherry — The famous Windows Solitaire victory cascade. Cards tumble and bounce across the screen in arcing trails. The reward animation that launched a thousand procrastinations.

MACHINES THAT CHANGED THE WORLD

Working models of machines that bent the arc of civilization — from ancient astronomical computers to the engines that powered the Industrial Revolution. Each one animates the actual mechanical principles: gears mesh, pistons fire, shuttles fly.

ANTIKYTHERA MECHANISM c. 100 BC — The first analog computer. An ancient Greek hand-powered orrery with interlocking bronze gears that predicted astronomical positions and eclipses. Nothing comparable was built for another 1,500 years.
ARCHIMEDES' SCREW c. 250 BC — A helical surface inside a cylinder, turned by hand to raise water. Still used today for irrigation and wastewater. 2,300 years of continuous service for one simple idea.
ASTROLABE c. 200 BC – 1600s — The “medieval smartphone.” A brass instrument that could tell time, find latitude, predict sunrise and sunset, and locate stars. Carried by astronomers and navigators for over a thousand years.
BEAM ENGINE 1769 — James Watt's separate condenser steam engine powered the Industrial Revolution. The iconic rocking beam links a piston and cylinder to a flywheel. The full four-stroke cycle, animated.
CAMSHAFT A rotating camshaft with eccentric lobes operating valve followers. The heartbeat of every internal combustion engine — converting rotation into precisely timed reciprocating motion.
CURTA CALCULATOR 1948 — Curt Herzstark's legendary handheld mechanical calculator, designed while imprisoned in Buchenwald. Cylindrical Leibniz wheels inside a pepper-grinder housing. Computation before electricity.
GRANDFATHER CLOCK 1656 — A longcase pendulum clock with escapement mechanism. The pendulum swings, the escape wheel ticks, the hands turn. Christiaan Huygens' invention that made accurate timekeeping possible.
GUTENBERG PRESS c. 1440 — The movable type printing press that democratized knowledge. The full mechanical cycle: inking, bed slide, press down, impression, release. Before Gutenberg, a book cost as much as a house.
GYROSCOPE A spinning rotor that resists changes to its orientation. Precession, nutation, and angular momentum made visible. The physics that keeps satellites pointed and bicycles upright.
JACQUARD LOOM 1804 — Punch cards controlling a weaving loom — the direct ancestor of computer programming. Ada Lovelace saw this machine and made the connection to general-purpose computation. A textile machine that led to software.
LOCOMOTIVE Side-view cutaway with driving wheels, coupling rods, and crankshaft. The crank-slider mechanism converts the piston's linear motion into rotary motion — the mechanical linkage that moved the world.
LOOM A working weaving loom. The shuttle passes through the warp threads, the heddles lift, the beater presses. Cloth forms row by row. Textile production at its most fundamental.
MODEL T 1908, Henry Ford — The car that put America on wheels. Animated cross-section showing the engine, transmission, and drive train. Mass production made visible in 64×64 pixels.
ORRERY 1713 — A clockwork model of the solar system. Brass arms carry planets at their correct relative orbital speeds, with gears visible at the base. Named after the 4th Earl of Orrery.
PROJECTOR A film projector with spinning reels, film threading through the gate, and a flickering beam of light. The mechanical poetry of cinema — 24 still images per second becoming motion.
SINGER A sewing machine with needle, bobbin, and feed dog. The needle plunges, the thread loops, the fabric advances. Isaac Singer's machine that changed the garment industry forever.
TYPEWRITER A mechanical typewriter with type bars striking an inked ribbon against paper. The carriage advances, the bell rings at the margin, the return lever sweeps back. QWERTY at its origin.
SWISS WATCH A five-gear train: barrel → center → third → fourth → escapement. Balance wheel with hairspring, proper gear meshing, and clock hands. Mechanical timekeeping in miniature.

MUSIC & SOUND

Instruments, audio visualizations, and the physics of sound — from waveforms on a virtual oscilloscope to the electromagnetic arcs of a theremin.

DRUM MACHINE A Roland TR-808-style step sequencer. 16 steps, multiple tracks for kick, snare, hi-hat, and more. The beat patterns scroll and animate. The machine that defined hip-hop, house, and electronic music.
EQUALIZER Stereo spectrum analyzer with 16 bouncing bars and peak-hold dots. Layered sine waves simulate kick, snare, and hi-hat. The LED bars that adorned every 1980s hi-fi system.
GRAMOPHONE 1887, Emile Berliner — A spinning disc, a tonearm, and a brass horn. The machine that made recorded music a mass medium. The platter turns, the arm traces the groove.
HURDY GURDY A medieval string instrument where a rosined wheel acts as a continuous bow. Keys press tangents against the strings. Drone notes sustain while the melody dances above. One of the oldest mechanical instruments.
JUKEBOX A classic coin-operated jukebox with visible record mechanism. The arm selects a disc, places it on the platter, and drops the needle. Neon lights and chrome — the American diner in miniature.
METRONOME A mechanical metronome with swinging pendulum and sliding weight. Tick-tock, tick-tock. The musician's timekeeper, setting the tempo for practice and performance.
MUSIC BOX A rotating cylinder with pins plucking a metal comb. Each pin triggers a note as the barrel turns. The original mechanical music player, producing delicate melodies from pure clockwork.
PIANO ROLL A scrolling piano roll showing notes in time. Colored bars descend onto a keyboard, playing a melody. The visual language of MIDI and digital music production, dating back to player pianos of the 1900s.
SYNTHESIZER A modular synthesizer with oscillators, filters, and patch cables. Waveforms morph on screen as parameters change. The instrument that created every electronic sound you've ever heard.
THEREMIN Léon Theremin, 1920 — The only instrument you play without touching. Side view with pitch antenna, volume loop, and electromagnetic field arcs. Waveform display shows the output.
TURNTABLE A vinyl record spinning with a tonearm tracking the groove. The platter rotates at 33 RPM, the stylus rides the spiral. The warm analog sound of vinyl, visualized.

THE NATURAL WORLD

Natural phenomena simulated from first principles. Fire rises, rain falls, stars recede, plasma swirls. Each effect is computed, not pre-rendered — the same physics that governs the real world, running on a grid of LEDs.

FIRE Classic fire simulation with a heat source at the bottom, upward propagation, and cooling. The algorithm dates back to the demoscene of the early 1990s. Simple math, primal result.
FIREFLIES Kuramoto model of coupled oscillators. Each firefly has its own internal clock, but when they can see their neighbors, they synchronize — just like real fireflies in Southeast Asia that flash in perfect unison.
PARTICLE LIFE Jeffrey Ventrella, 2007 — Multiple species with asymmetric attraction and repulsion forces. Particles self-organize into orbits, chains, and structures that look eerily like living organisms.
JELLYFISH A Particle Life variant tuned for drifting, pulsing jellyfish motion. Species interact with asymmetric forces that produce translucent bell shapes trailing luminous tentacles through dark water.
LAKE A serene lake surface with ripples, reflections, and gentle wave motion. Pixels shimmer as if catching light on water. Digital tranquility.
PLASMA Four overlapping sine functions create smoothly animated color fields — one of the quintessential demoscene effects. The math is trivial. The result is hypnotic.
RIPPLES Concentric rings expand from random drop points, interact, and fade. A rain-on-water simulation using the 2D wave equation with damping. Interference patterns form where rings collide.
STARFIELD 3D stars rushing toward you with perspective projection. The classic warp-speed effect: dots start small in the center and streak outward as they approach. Infinite depth from 2D math.
WEATHER Animated weather scenes: rain, snow, thunderstorms, sunshine. Particles fall with realistic physics, lightning flashes illuminate the grid. A miniature sky.

ROAD & RAIL

Traffic simulations and transportation networks. Cars flow through intersections, merge onto highways, and navigate roundabouts. Each simulation models real traffic dynamics — congestion waves, signal timing, and the emergent behavior of vehicles following simple rules.

CLOVERLEAF A highway interchange with looping ramps. Cars flow through the cloverleaf pattern, merging and diverging. The elegant (if land-hungry) solution to crossing traffic without stopping.
GREEN WAVE Traffic signals timed so that a car traveling at the speed limit hits green lights all the way down a corridor. The dream of every urban traffic engineer, animated.
GRIDLOCK Biham, Middleton & Levine, 1992 — The BML traffic model. Cars move east or north on alternating steps. Below a critical density, traffic flows. Above it, the system jams completely. A phase transition you can see.
HIGHWAY Multi-lane highway traffic with lane changes, speed differences, and phantom traffic jams. Congestion waves propagate backward through the flow — the accordion effect made visible.
ON-RAMP Cars merging from a ramp onto a flowing highway. Gap acceptance, yield behavior, and the cascade effects of a single slow merge. The daily anxiety of highway driving, simulated.
ROAD A single road with cars following each other. Car-following models produce stop-and-go waves from nothing — no accident, no bottleneck, just the physics of human reaction times.
ROTARY A traffic roundabout with entering and circulating vehicles. Yield-on-entry dynamics create a self-regulating flow. The European alternative to the four-way stop.
SIGNALS A signalized intersection with timed red/green phases. Cars queue, the light changes, the queue discharges. Signal timing optimization — the invisible engineering that keeps cities moving.

SCIENCE

Physics, chemistry, and biology on 4,096 pixels. These simulations solve real equations in real time: Navier-Stokes for fluids, Coulomb's law for electric fields, the wave equation for diffraction. Nothing is faked — every dot on screen comes from the math.

STRANGE ATTRACTORS Edward Lorenz, 1963 — The Lorenz “butterfly” attractor, the Rössler attractor, and the Thomas attractor. Trajectories that fold forever through space without ever crossing themselves. The birth of chaos theory.
NEURONS Izhikevich model — A spiking neural network with excitatory and inhibitory neurons. Regular spiking, chattering, and bursting patterns. Membrane potential dynamics with real spike generation.
CELL A data-driven cross-section of a living cell. 14 real biochemical pathways: ATP synthase spinning, ribosomes translating mRNA, protein complexes cycling through conformational states.
CHLADNI PLATES Ernst Chladni, 1787 — Sprinkle sand on a vibrating plate and it collects at the nodes, revealing standing wave patterns. Each frequency produces a unique geometric figure. The shapes of sound.
COLOR MIX Computational fluid dynamics with colored dyes mixing in a flow field. Watch pigments swirl, fold, and blend according to Navier-Stokes. Stir the fluid and see chaos create beauty.
ELECTROMAGNETISM A Coulomb electrostatics sandbox with field-line tracing and equipotential rendering. Place charges and watch the fields form: dipoles, quadrupoles, circuits. Every field line from the inverse-square law.
DOUBLE PENDULUM The simplest physical system that produces chaos. Two arms, two masses, deterministic equations — yet the trajectory never repeats. Sensitive dependence on initial conditions, made visible.
DNA The central dogma of molecular biology, animated. Six scenarios: double helix structure, replication, transcription, codon reading, mutation, and chromatin packing.
EARTH A rotating globe showing continents, oceans, and the terminator (day/night boundary). The planet as seen from space, rendered on a 64×64 pixel sphere.
TURING PATTERNS Alan Turing, 1952 — In his final paper, Turing proposed that chemical reactions could explain leopard spots. The Gray-Scott model produces spots, stripes, coral, and worms — all from one equation with different parameters.
INK DROPS Drops of colored ink falling into a fluid simulation. Watch the ink spread, curl, and diffuse according to real fluid dynamics. Computational beauty from Navier-Stokes.
CRYSTAL LATTICES 3D rotating unit cells of real crystalline structures: diamond, salt, ice, metals. Tiled cells with edge fading show infinite periodicity. The atomic geometry of solid matter.
MICROSCOPE Scenes viewed through a virtual microscope: microorganisms, cell structures, and crystal formations. The hidden world of the very small, magnified to fill the LED grid.
MOLECULES 3D ball-and-stick models with CPK coloring (carbon black, oxygen red, nitrogen blue). Real molecules rotating in space: amino acids, sugars, neurotransmitters.
OPTICS Geometric ray tracing from first principles. Light enters a prism and splits into spectral colors at their correct refractive indices. Other scenarios show lenses, mirrors, and kaleidoscopes.
OSCILLOSCOPE Phosphor-green vintage oscilloscope with sine, square, triangle, and sawtooth waveforms. Lissajous mode draws figures from frequency ratios — the same patterns used to calibrate TV broadcasts.
PERIODIC TABLE Animated Bohr models for all 118 elements. Electrons orbit on shells, nuclei pulse. Element categories color-coded: alkali metals, noble gases, transition metals, lanthanides.
PROTEINS Real protein backbone structures from the RCSB Protein Data Bank. Cα coordinates with color-coded secondary structure: helices in red, sheets in yellow, coils in gray.
RADIOACTIVE DECAY A grid of atoms spontaneously decays, emitting alpha, beta, and gamma particles. Five scenarios demonstrate different decay modes. Each event is probabilistic — quantum randomness made visible.
SOLAR SYSTEM Newtonian gravity with Velocity Verlet integration. Planets orbit at correct relative speeds around a central star. The clockwork of the heavens, computed in real time.
SPACE DUST An asteroid belt of particles orbiting under gravity. Rocks drift, collide, and cluster. Softened gravitational potential prevents singularities at close approach.
SPECTROSCOPE Real element emission spectra at their correct wavelengths. Each element has a unique fingerprint of spectral lines — this is how we know what distant stars are made of.
WAVE TANK The 2D wave equation solved with leapfrog integration. Point sources, double-slit diffraction, two-source interference, ripples, and reflection. Real wave physics on a grid.
WIND TUNNEL Jos Stam, SIGGRAPH 1999 — Computational fluid dynamics with obstacles. Semi-Lagrangian advection shows vortex shedding, turbulence, and flow separation around objects. Real aerodynamics at 64×64.
3 BODY PROBLEM Three bodies orbiting under mutual gravitational attraction. No closed-form solution exists — the system is inherently chaotic. Watch them dance, fling each other away, and sometimes find temporary stability.

HOW IT WORKS

A physical machine. No screen, no monitor — just raw LEDs and a single-board computer.

DISPLAY 64×64 RGB LED matrix
HUB75 interface
4,096 individually addressable LEDs
COMPUTER Raspberry Pi 3
1.2 GHz ARM Cortex-A53
1 GB RAM
SOFTWARE Python 3
~40,000 lines of code
Every effect built from scratch
INPUT Joystick + 2 buttons
Wired to GPIO pins
Hold both to exit
No GPU. No shaders. No numpy on the Pi. Every pixel is computed in Python on a 1.2 GHz ARM chip. Fluid dynamics, ray tracing, reaction-diffusion, N-body gravity — all running at 30fps on hardware that costs $35. The constraint forces algorithmic creativity: when you can't brute-force it, you have to be clever.
